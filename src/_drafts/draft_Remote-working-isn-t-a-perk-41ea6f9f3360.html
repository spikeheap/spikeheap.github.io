Remote working isn’t a perk

If you’re offering remote working a couple of days a week, you might just be doing it all wrong

Trawling the “traditional” job boards, there’s an emerging pattern of offering remote work as a perk:

This company also offers up to two days working from home and flexible working hours!

I’ve been digging into this with recruiters recently, and it turns out this is a response to the challenge of finding and retaining good developers, both in contract and perm roles. Companies are being forced to find new ways to differentiate themselves to candidates, and quite a few recruiters have helped these companies realise that they can widen the net by offering remote aspects of work. All in all, this is a pretty promising progression.

There’s just one problem: companies are starting to embrace remote as a perk, rather than a way to manage operations, and from what I’ve seen this leads to a mismatch of expectations with candidates, an environment where remote working is less sustainable, and a conflict of objectives.

By making it a perk, you feel like you’re doing the candidate a favour

If we approach remote as something to minimise, there’s always going to be a tension between the employee/contractor and the company. I’ve had conversations with recruiters along the lines of “how many days would need to be remote for this role to be right for you”. The answer to that is always the same: 

What would I be doing that would require me to be in the office? Travelling in for meetings, planning events, hack days, retros, etc. is absolutely fine, but sitting on a train for two hours one day to sit at a desk when I did the same work remotely the day before doesn’t make much sense.

When remote working is used as a bargaining chip in this way, it comes across as being about having bums-on-seats, rather than empowering people to do their best work from the right place.

What is the right place?

Everyone’s different, and what’s right may change over time. Some of the most talented developers I’ve worked with have really struggled to concentrate in an open-plan office environment with all the distractions and noise that that can entail, and switching to a home office has made the world of difference to their happiness and productivity. Others have had the opposite, where working remotely meant late mornings and a constant connection to work which ground them down, but they thrived being able to silo work by travelling home at the end of the day. The right place can even change for a single person over time — I find the quiet, distraction-free environment of my home-office, with the hipster standing desk/iMac setup ideal for architecting and writing code, but I find it easier to write prose with the bustle of a coffee shop in the background.

The key is to empower people to identify what works best for them, and make the most of it.

Great, but we have a lot of face-to-face meetings

Some things are undoubtedly easier face-to-face, especially at the beginning of your journey to remote working. However it’s also a common cop-out to save us having to think about how we make the company work for remote workers. Meetings are an obvious example of where you might expect people to be in the office, but are there other ways remote workers might lose out? Are architectural decisions made over coffee in the kitchen? Does the project manager come over and ask how people are doing? If you’re not replicating this into channels where remote workers can take part, they’ll likely feel excluded, and if they’re part remote that means they’re more likely to travel into the office more so they don’t miss out. With more people in the office more often the effects are compounded until remote working is just something people do when they have someone coming over to fix the boiler one day.
